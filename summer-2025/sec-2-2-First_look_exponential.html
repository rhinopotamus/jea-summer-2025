    Subsection Percents of different wholes

    Jocelyn got a job right out of college, as an administrative assistant earning $28,000 a year. The position turned out to be a great fit for her, and after one year she was promoted to data analyst with a 15% raise. The next year Joceyln was promoted again, to senior data analyst along with a 21% raise. ‚ÄúNot bad,‚Äù her friend Audun said, ‚Äúa 36% raise in two years.‚Äù But Jocelyn quickly corrected him. ‚ÄúAudun, it‚Äôs even better than that! It‚Äôs over 39%!‚Äù
    üîó
    After the first year, Jocelyn‚Äôs salary of $28,000 was increased by 15%. Remember that means that her raise was 15% of $28,000. To calculate 15% of $28,000 we multiply using the decimal form
    \begin{equation*} 15\% = \frac{15}{100} = 15 \div 100 = 0.15 \end{equation*}
    to get
    \begin{equation*} 15\% \text{ of } 28{,}000 = 0.15 \times 28{,}000 = 4{,}200 \end{equation*}
    That‚Äôs how much Jocelyn‚Äôs raise was that first year. By adding that amount to the original salary we get
    \begin{equation*} 28{,}000 + 4{,}200 = 32{,}200 \end{equation*}
    After one year Jocelyn‚Äôs salary was $32,200.
    üîó
    After the second year, Jocelyn got a 21% raise. But 21% of what? This means her salary rose by 21% from what it was just before the raise, that is, from the $32,200. (The 21% does not refer back to the original $28,000 value!) So, to calculate the second increase, we take 21% of $32,200, which is
    \begin{equation*} 21\% \text{ of } \$32{,}200 = 0.21 \times 32{,}200 = \$6{,}762 \end{equation*}
    By adding on this raise we get
    \begin{equation*} 32{,}200+ 6{,}762 = 38{,}962 \end{equation*}
    After the second year Jocelyn was earning $38,962.
    üîó
    Since Jocelyn‚Äôs original salary was $28,000, the net increase in her salary is the difference
    \begin{equation*} \$38{,}962 - \$28{,}000 = \$10{,}962 \end{equation*}
    The corresponding percentage increase was
    \begin{equation*} \frac{10{,}962}{28{,}000}= 10{,}962 \div 28{,}000 = 0.3915 = 0.3915 \times 100\% = 39.15\% \end{equation*}
    As Jocelyn said, that‚Äôs an increase of over 39% of her original salary.
    üîó
    What‚Äôs going on here? Audun thought that 15% and 21% would be 36% because \(15 + 21 = 36\text{.}\) The reason it doesn‚Äôt work that way is that while the 15% is of the original $28,000, the 21% is of the $32,200. So, we can‚Äôt just combine percentages by adding, because the wholes might be different.
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection A one-step process for finding percent change

    Each time we figured out Jocelyn‚Äôs new salary, we did a two-step process. First, we calculated the amount of the increase, and second, we found the new value by adding on. There‚Äôs actually an easier way.
    üîó
    Jocelyn‚Äôs salary was $28,000 and then went up by 15%. For her new salary we want to add her old salary (all of it) plus 15%. So we want 100% of her old salary plus 15% of her old salary. Since we are finding two percents of the same whole, we can add the percents together: in this case, we can compute 115% of her old salary.
    üîó
    That works in general. When we increase a number by 15%, we end up with 100% of what we started with plus 15% more of what we started with, for a grand total of 115% of what we started with. So we can just multiply by 1.15, which is 115% written in decimal, since
    \begin{equation*} 115\% = \frac{115}{100} = 115 \div 100 = 1.15 \end{equation*}
    Looks weird, works great.
    üîó
    That means all we have to do to find Jocelyn‚Äôs salary is
    \begin{equation*} 28{,}000 \times 1.15 = 32{,}200 \end{equation*}
    We can do the same thing for the next calculation:
    \begin{equation*} 32{,}200 \times 1.21 = 38{,}962 \end{equation*}
    Here we multiplied by 1.21 because after a 21% increase you have 121% of what you started with. And 121% in decimal form is just 1.21.
    üîó
    Now hang on to your hat, because we can combine these parts together. In our example, we started with $28,000. Then we multiplied by 1.15, which gave us $32,200. And then we multiplied that answer by 1.21, to get our final answer of $28,962. So really we just did
    \begin{equation*} 28{,}000 \times 1.15 \times 1.21 = 38{,}962 \end{equation*}
    Same answer. A lot less effort. And, check it out:
    \begin{equation*} 1.15 \times 1.21 = 1.3915 =139.15\% \end{equation*}
    That‚Äôs where the 39.15% increase is hidden. Cool.
    üîó

    Subsection Growth rate and growth factor

    Here‚Äôs a little vocabulary that will be very helpful for us moving forward. A percentage increase is known as the growth rate and the number we multiply in the one-step method is called the growth factor.
    A good way to remember why it is called the growth factor is that a factor is something that is multiplied, and the growth factor is the number we multiply by in the one-step method.
    üîó
    For example, in calculating 15% increase, the growth rate was \(15\%=0.15\) in decimal, and so the growth factor was
    \begin{equation*} 115\%=1.15 \end{equation*}
    If you‚Äôre into formulas here it is.
    üîó

    Percent Change Formula

    If a quantity increases by a percentage corresponding to growth rate \(r\text{,}\) then the growth factor is
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle g=1+r \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    For Jocelyn‚Äôs 15% raise, we had \(r=0.15\) and so
    \begin{equation*} g=1+r=1+0.15=1.15 \end{equation*}
    Or, in percent format, we had \(r=15\%\) and so
    \begin{equation*} g=100\% + r = 100\% + 15\% = 115\% \end{equation*}
    üîó
    Let‚Äôs summarize all of this in a table, so you can see the connections between \(r\) and \(g\) in both the percent format and the decimal format.
    üîó
    Percent format Decimal format
    Growth rate \(r\) \(15\%\) \(0.15\)
    Growth factor \(g\) \(115\%\) \(1.15\)

    Subsection Using exponents to find multiple increases at once

    Jocelyn‚Äôs most recent assignment has been analyzing information on rising health care costs. In 2007 the United States spent $2.26 trillion on health care. Written out with all its zeros that‚Äôs
    \begin{equation*} \$2.26 \text{ trillion} = \$2{,}260{,}000{,}000{,}000 \end{equation*}
    Health care costs were projected to increase at an average of 6.7% annually. That means we have
    \begin{equation*} r= 6.7\% = 0.067 \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} g=1+r=1+0.067=1.067 \end{equation*}
    So, to find the effect of a 6.7% increase, we can just multiply by 1.067. Again, that‚Äôs the 100% of what we started with plus 6.7% more for a grand total of \(106.7\%= 1.067\text{.}\)
    üîó
    We are ready to do some examples. In 2007 the United States spent $2.26 trillion on health care. The projection for one year later is
    \begin{equation*} 2.26 \times 1.067 = 2.41142 \approx \$2.41 \text{ trillion} \end{equation*}
    Another year later, projected health care costs are
    \begin{equation*} 2.41 \times 1.067 = 2.57147 \approx \$2.57 \text{ trillion} \end{equation*}
    And so on. For each year we multiply by another 1.067.
    üîó
    For example, by 2017 (ten years later), health care costs are projected to be
    \begin{equation*} 2.26 \times \underbrace{1.067 \times 1.067 \times 1.067 \times 1.067 \times 1.067 \times 1.067 \times 1.067 \times 1.067 \times 1.067 \times 1.067}_{10\text{ times}} \end{equation*}
    I don‚Äôt know about you, but I would rather not type that all into a calculator ‚Äî I‚Äôm worried that I would lose count of how many times I typed 1.067. Luckily, multiplying by 1.067 ten times is the same as multiplying by \(1.067^{10}\text{.}\) Recall that the base \(1.067\) is the number we multiply by and the exponent (or power) \(10\) tells us how many times. So we can calculate
    \begin{equation*} 2.26 \ast 1.067^{10} = 2.26 \times 1.067 \wedge 10 = 4.322675488 \approx \$ 4.32 \text{ trillion} \end{equation*}
    Notice that the order of operations is exactly what we wanted here: first raise 1.067 to the 10th power, then multiply by 2.26. So we can enter it all at once without needing parentheses. Bottom line: health care costs are expected to be around $4.32 trillion by the year 2017. Oh my!
    üîó

    Subsection Generalizing to an equation

    We‚Äôre so close to the equation for Jocelyn‚Äôs health care costs assignment now, we can smell it. Our variables are
    \begin{align*} H \amp= \text{ health care costs (\$ trillions) } \sim \text{ dep} \\ Y \amp= \text{ time (years since 2007) } \sim \text{ indep} \end{align*}
    We just found the cost after 10 years was
    \begin{equation*} \$2.26 \ast 1.067^{10}\approx \$4.32 \text{ trillion} \end{equation*}
    We can generalize to get the equation by putting in \(Y\) (instead of 10) and \(H\) (instead of $4.32 trillion). When we do, we get
    \begin{equation*} 2.26 \ast 1.067^{Y} = H \end{equation*}
    Rewriting the equation to begin with the dependent variable we get
    \begin{equation*} H = 2.26\ast 1.067^{Y} \end{equation*}
    By the way, there are two other standard ways of writing this equation
    \begin{equation*} H= 2.26(1.067)^{Y} \quad \text{ and } \quad H = 2.26\left(1.067^{Y}\right) \end{equation*}
    üîó
    For example, in 2027 we have \(Y=2027 - 2007 = 20\) years and our equation projects health care cost at
    \begin{equation*} 2.26 \ast 1.067^{20} = 2.26 \times 1.067 \wedge 20 = 8.821882053 \approx \$8.82 \text{ trillion} \end{equation*}
    Not sure it‚Äôs realistic to expect that steady an increase for 20 years, but that is what the equation projects, so let‚Äôs work with it for now.
    üîó
    What does the graph of this function look like? Here are the values we found so far. That should be enough.
    üîó
    year 2007 2008 2009 2017 2027
    \(Y\) 0 1 2 10 20
    \(H\) 2.26 2.41 2.57 4.32 8.82
    Can you see that the graph curves slightly? It‚Äôs not a straight line. That‚Äôs because this function isn‚Äôt linear.

    Subsection A template for exponential equations

    Look back at our equation
    \begin{equation*} H = 2.26\ast 1.067^{Y} \end{equation*}
    This type of equation is called an exponential equation because the independent variable is in the exponent. Any exponential equation fits this template.
    üîó

    Exponential Equation Template

    \begin{equation*} \text{dep} = \text{start} \ast \text{growth factor} ^ {\text{indep}} \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    Notice our two variables are in our equation and there are two constants. Each constant has its own meaning. The first constant is 2.26 and it is measured in trillions of dollars. It is the amount spent on health care in the starting year of 2007. In our standard form we refer to this quantity as the starting value (or start for short). As with linear equations, its official name is intercept and it‚Äôs the value where the curve crosses the vertical axis on the graph.
    üîó
    The second constant is the growth factor 1.067, and is the decimal equivalent of the 106.7%. The growth factor for an exponential equation is similar to the slope of a linear equation because both tell us how fast the function is increasing. But the slope measures the rate of change ‚Äî how much is added at each step ‚Äî while the growth factor corresponds to the percent increase. Another way to say that is linear functions correspond to situations where we are adding the same amount each time and exponential functions correspond to situations where we are adding the same percentage each time (or, equivalently, multiplying by the same amount each time).
    üîó
    Sometimes the graph of an exponential equation looks a lot like a straight line, especially if you only plot a few points. A good rule of thumb is to plot five or more points to see the curve in the graph of an exponential equation.
