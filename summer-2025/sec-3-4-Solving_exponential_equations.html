    Subsection Solving an exponential equation symbolically

    Successive approximation gives us the answer fairly quickly. But there is an even quicker way â€” solving the exponential equation using the Shoes and Socks Principle. Start with what weâ€™re looking for, which is \(H= 3\text{.}\) Use the equation \(H=2.26\ast1.067^Y\) to get
    \begin{equation*} 2.26\ast1.067^Y=3\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Opposite steps in the opposite order: we want to find the value of \(Y\text{,}\) so divide each side by 2.26 to get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\cancel{2.26}\ast1.067^Y}{\cancel{2.26}}= \frac{3} {2.26} \end{equation*}
    which simplifies to
    \begin{equation*} 1.067^Y=\frac{3}{2.26}=3 \div 2.26 = 1.327433628\ldots \end{equation*}
    When the dust settles weâ€™re left with
    \begin{equation*} 1.067^Y = 1.327433628\ldots\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Hmm. How do we find \(Y\) here? We saw how to use roots to solve power equations. In our lemonade example we had \(C^3 = 714.2857\ldots\text{.}\) We knew the exponent (3) and wanted to find the number being raised to that power (\(C\)). Thatâ€™s when we took the cube root to get
    \begin{equation*} C = \sqrt[3]{714.2857\ldots} =3 \sqrt[x]{~\text{ }} 714.2857 = 8.9390\ldots \approx 8.9 \text{ inches}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    That approach is not going to work here because the operation is backwards now - we know the number being raised to a power (1.067) and are on the hunt for the exponent (\(Y\)) instead.
    ðŸ”—
    Turns out thereâ€™s a different formula for solving for the exponent that uses logarithms (nickname: logs). More about logs in a minute, but first letâ€™s write down the formula and practice working with it. The formula is

    Log-Divides Formula

    The equation \(g^Y = v\) has solution
    \begin{equation*} Y = \frac{\log (v)}{\log(g)}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(g\) is the growth factor and \(v\) is the value.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Quick aside about the name. Some formulas have well-known names. Not this one. We call it the â€œLog-Divides Formulaâ€ because it has logs and divides in it. Perhaps you already guessed that. Other math books do not have a name for this formula, although it is related to something called the â€œchange of base formulaâ€.
    ðŸ”—
    Okay. Back to solving our equation. We got stuck trying to solve
    \begin{equation*} 1.067^Y = 1.327433628\ldots \end{equation*}
    We have growth factor \(g = 1.067\) and value \(v=1.327433628\ldots\text{.}\) So the formula says
    \begin{align*} Y \amp = \frac{\log (v)}{\log(g)} \\ \amp = \frac{\log (1.327433628\ldots)}{\log(1.067)} \\ \amp = \log (1.327433628\ldots) \div \log (1.067) \\ \amp = 4.367667365 \approx 4.37 \end{align*}
    Your calculator should have a key that says log or maybe LOG. Try typing
    \begin{equation*} \log( 1.327433628) \div \log (1.067) = 4.367667365 \approx 4.37\text{.} \end{equation*}
    A small note here about parentheses. Some calculators give the first parenthesis for free when you type log but you have to type the closing parenthesis in yourself.
    ðŸ”—
    This answer of 4.37 means that costs are projected to exceed $3 trillion just over 4 years after 2007. Thatâ€™s some time during 2011, or by 2012 for sure. Same answer as before.

    Subsection Another example

    Letâ€™s do another example for practice. Suppose instead we want to know when health care costs would exceed $10 trillion instead. (By the way - wow!) That means \(H = 10\text{.}\) Using our equation \(H=2.26\ast1.067^Y\) we get
    \begin{equation*} 2.26\ast1.067^Y=10\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Opposite steps in the opposite order: first we should divide boty sides by 2.26.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\cancel{2.26}\ast1.067^Y}{\cancel{2.26}}=\frac{10}{2.26}=10 \div 2.26 = 4.424778761\ldots \end{equation*}
    That means
    \begin{equation*} 1.067^Y=4.424778761\ldots \end{equation*}
    Now our equation fits the format \(g^Y=v\) for the Log-Divides Formula with new value \(v=4.424778761\ldots\) (and the growth factor is \(g=1.067\) still). So the answer is
    \begin{align*} Y \amp = \frac{\log (v)}{\log(g)} \\ \amp = \frac{\log (4.424778761\ldots)}{\log(1.067)} \\ \amp = \log (4.424778761\ldots) \div \log (1.067) \\ \amp = 22.932891 \approx 23 \end{align*}
    Want to avoid typing in the number 4.424778761â€¦? Depending on your calculator, you might try this instead:
    \begin{equation*} 10 \div 2.26 = \log(\text{ANS}) \div \log(1.067)= 22.932891 \end{equation*}
    where ANS stands for â€œanswerâ€.

    Subsection Logarithms

    Time to fill you in a bit more about logs. Look at these examples. Donâ€™t take my word for it; calculate them yourself.
    \begin{align*} \log(10) \amp =1\\ \log(100) \amp =2\\ \log(1{,}000) \amp =3\\ \log(10{,}000) \amp= 4 \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    What do you see? In each case the logarithm is the number of zeros. For example, has 4 zeros and \(\log(10{,}000)=4\text{.}\) Another way to think of this connection is
    \begin{equation*} 10{,}000 = 10^4 \text{ and } \log(10{,}000)=4\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In other words, the logarithm is picking off the power of 10.
    ðŸ”—
    Wait a minute. The Log-Divides formula helped us find the value of \(Y\) which was an exponent. And now we see that the log of a power of 10 is that exponent. So a logarithm is just an exponent. And logarithms help us find the exponent. Makes sense.
    ðŸ”—
    What about logs of numbers that arenâ€™t just powers of 10? Here are some examples.
    \begin{align*} \log(25) \amp = 1.3979\ldots \\ \log(250) \amp = 2.3979\ldots \\ \log(2{,}500) \amp = 3.3979\ldots \\ \log(25{,}000) \amp = 4.3979\ldots \end{align*}
    ðŸ”—
    To see whatâ€™s happening we want to involve powers of 10. Scientific notation will do that for us. Letâ€™s write these numbers in scientific notation and see what we learn. For example,
    \begin{equation*} 25{,}000 = 2.5 \times 10^4 \text{ and } \log( 25{,}000)=4.3979\ldots \approx 4\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We are back to the power of 10. Well, approximately. Letâ€™s check another number.
    \begin{equation*} 250 = 2.5 \times 10^2 \qquad \text{ and } \log(250) = 2.3979\ldots \approx 2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Before we write down a general rule, letâ€™s check more numbers.
    \begin{align*} 7{,}420{,}000 \amp= 7.42 \times 10^6 \amp \amp \text{ and } \amp \log(7{,}420{,}000) \amp=6.870403905\ldots \approx 6\\ 4 \amp= 4\times 10^0 \amp \amp \text{ and } \amp \log(4) \amp= 0.602059991\ldots \approx 0\\ 0.00917 \amp= 9.17\times 10^{-3} \amp \amp \text{ and } \amp \log(0.00917) \amp= -2.037630664\ldots \approx -3 \end{align*}
    In every case we are rounding down, but itâ€™s always the same.
    ðŸ”—
    log(number) \(\approx\) power of 10 in the scientific notation for that number.
